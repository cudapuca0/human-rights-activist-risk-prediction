## The Story Behind This Project

In the summer of 2025, I moved from Canada to Frankfurt, Germany, to join the Goethe Research Experience Program (GREP) and work at the Peace Research Institute Frankfurt (PRIF).

As someone who grew up in Latin America, I have always been aware of the challenges faced by activists, journalists, environmental defenders, Indigenous leaders, and community organizers. Many of these individuals dedicate their lives to protecting their communities, yet some face threats, violence, or even death because of their work.

I began wondering whether there were broader patterns behind these tragedies.

Why are some countries consistently more dangerous for human rights defenders than others?

Are these incidents isolated events, or are there deeper political, economic, and institutional factors that place certain groups at greater risk?

This project became my attempt to answer those questions using data.

Over several months, I merged international datasets, built statistical models, tested machine learning algorithms, and spent countless hours trying to understand what separates countries where defenders are relatively safe from those where they face extreme danger.

What started as a summer research project became one of the most meaningful analytical experiences of my academic journey.

While this repository contains models, code, and data analysis, it is ultimately about people.

---

## Project Overview

This project investigates killings of Human Rights Defenders (HRDs) between 2014 and 2020 using data from around the world.

The objective was not only to identify where violence occurs, but also to better understand the political, economic, institutional, and social conditions associated with increased risk.

The analysis focuses on both global and Latin American patterns, allowing for comparisons between worldwide trends and the unique dynamics of one of the regions most affected by violence against defenders.

## The Research That Inspired Me

One of the most rewarding parts of this project was discovering how much excellent research already exists on human rights defenders, political violence, and governance. These papers shaped many of the questions I asked and influenced the methods I chose throughout the project.

### Environmental and Land Defenders

**Le Billon, P., & Lujala, P. (2020)**  
*Environmental and Land Defenders: Global Patterns and Determinants of Repression*

This was one of the first papers I read when starting the project. It explores how natural resource extraction, Indigenous land claims, and foreign investment intersect with violence against environmental defenders. It encouraged me to think beyond individual incidents and focus on the broader systems that place activists at risk.

### Human Rights Defenders and Political Repression

**Krain, M., Murdie, A., & Beard, A. (2024)**  
*Silencing Human Rights Defenders Once and for All? Determinants of Human Rights Defenders' Killings*

This paper became one of the main foundations of my research. The authors examine how state repression, civic space restrictions, and institutional weakness influence violence against human rights defenders. Many of the variables explored in my models were inspired by questions raised in this study.

### Longitudinal Data Analysis

**Liang, K. Y., & Zeger, S. L. (1986)**  
*Longitudinal Data Analysis Using Generalized Linear Models*

This landmark paper introduced Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE), the primary statistical framework used throughout this project. Despite being published decades ago, it remains one of the most influential contributions to longitudinal data analysis.

## Methodology

I wanted to approach the problem from both an explanatory and predictive perspective.

### Statistical Analysis

To understand relationships between variables and identify statistically significant predictors, I used:

- Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE)
- Negative Binomial Regression
- Longitudinal Data Analysis
- Correlation Structure Selection
- Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) Screening
- QIC-Based Model Selection

### Machine Learning

To complement the statistical analysis and capture nonlinear relationships, I also implemented:

- Random Forest Regression
- Feature Importance Analysis
- Predictive Modeling
- RMSE Evaluation
- R² Performance Assessment

By combining econometric methods with machine learning, I was able to balance interpretability and predictive performance while exploring complex interactions in the data.

---

## Key Findings

Several patterns emerged consistently across models.

Factors associated with higher levels of violence against human rights defenders included:

- State repression
- Political killings
- Torture
- Institutional weaknesses
- Youth unemployment
- Demographic pressures

Meanwhile, countries with:

- Greater access to justice
- Equal protection under the law
- More inclusive political institutions

tended to experience fewer killings.

One of the most interesting findings was that Latin America displayed several patterns that differed from the global sample, highlighting the importance of regional context when studying violence, governance, and social conflict.

## What I Learned

One of the biggest lessons from this project is that data rarely tells the entire story on its own. Many of the countries where defenders face the greatest risks also have limited reporting systems, restricted press freedom, or weak institutional transparency. In those contexts, missing data does not necessarily mean the absence of violence. Sometimes it means the opposite 
Working on this project taught me to think more critically about data quality, measurement challenges, and the responsibility analysts have when interpreting evidence.
